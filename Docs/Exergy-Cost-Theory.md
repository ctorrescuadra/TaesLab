# Exergy Cost Theory

## Introduction

The **Exergy Cost Theory (ECT)** is the mathematical foundation of thermoeconomic analysis in TaesLab. It provides a systematic method for calculating the cost of products in energy systems by considering both exergy destruction (irreversibilities) and external costs (capital, operation, maintenance, environmental impacts).

The theory is based on an operator formalism that allows recursive cost propagation through the productive structure of the system.

## Fundamental Concepts

### Unit Consumption

The **unit consumption** $k_p$ of a process $p$ is defined as the ratio of fuel to product exergy:

$$k_p = \frac{F_p}{P_p}$$

where:
- $F_p$ is the fuel exergy consumed by process $p$
- $P_p$ is the product exergy generated by process $p$

For a **reversible process**, $k_p = 1$. For **irreversible processes**, $k_p > 1$.

The **irreversibility** of process $p$ can be expressed as:

$$I_p = F_p - P_p = P_p(k_p - 1)$$

### Exergy Efficiency

The **exergy efficiency** $\varepsilon_p$ of a process is the inverse of unit consumption:

$$\varepsilon_p = \frac{1}{k_p} = \frac{P_p}{F_p}$$

## Matrix Operators

### Flow Operators

The **flow recycling matrix** $\mathbf{G}$ captures the internal flow relationships:

$$\mathbf{G} = \mathbf{F}_{(:,1:N)} \mathbf{P}_{(1:N,:)} + \mathbf{V}$$

where:
- $\mathbf{F}$ is the flow-to-fuel incidence matrix
- $\mathbf{P}$ is the product-to-flow incidence matrix
- $\mathbf{V}$ is the flow dissipation matrix
- $N$ is the number of productive processes

The **flow balance operator** $\mathbf{B}$ propagates costs through the flow network:

$$\mathbf{B} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{G})^{-1}$$

This operator exists when $\rho(\mathbf{G}) < 1$ (see [M-Matrix characterization](M-Matrix.md)).

### Process Operators

The **normalized fuel matrix** $\mathbf{P}_F$ is obtained by normalizing the fuel-product table:

$$\mathbf{P}_F = \frac{\text{FP\_table}}{\text{FuelExergy}}$$

The **unit consumption matrix** $\mathbf{K}_P$ relates process products to fuels:

$$\mathbf{K}_P = \mathbf{P}_F \cdot \text{diag}(\mathbf{k})$$

where $\mathbf{k} = [k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_n]^T$ is the vector of unit consumptions.

The **process cost propagation operator** $\mathbf{P}$ is the fundamental operator in ECT:

$$\mathbf{P} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K}_P)^{-1}$$

This operator distributes costs from resources through all productive processes. The series expansion reveals the cost propagation chain:

$$\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{I} + \mathbf{K}_P + \mathbf{K}_P^2 + \mathbf{K}_P^3 + \cdots$$

The **irreversibility operator** $\mathbf{I}_{\text{op}}$ quantifies cost accumulation due to irreversibilities:

$$\mathbf{I}_{\text{op}} = \mathbf{P} - \mathbf{I}$$

The **normalized product matrix** $\mathbf{F}_P$ is:

$$\mathbf{F}_P = \frac{\text{FP\_table}}{\text{ProductExergy}}$$

The **product cost propagation with circulation** $\mathbf{C}_P$ accounts for feedback loops:

$$\mathbf{C}_P = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{F}_P)^{-1}$$

## Exergy Cost Calculations

### Total Process Costs

The total costs are distributed across all processes:

**Exergy-based cost** (direct exergy cost):
$$\mathbf{C}^E_P = \mathbf{c}_e \cdot \mathbf{C}_P$$

where $\mathbf{c}_e$ is the vector of resource exergy costs.

**External cost** (capital, O&M, environmental):
$$\mathbf{C}^Z_P = \mathbf{Z} \cdot \mathbf{C}_P$$

where $\mathbf{Z}$ is the vector of external costs assigned to processes.

**Waste recycling cost**:
$$\mathbf{C}^R_P = (\mathbf{C}^E_P + \mathbf{C}^Z_P) \cdot \mathbf{R}$$

where $\mathbf{R}$ is the waste recycling operator.

**Total product cost**:
$$\mathbf{C}_P = \mathbf{C}^E_P + \mathbf{C}^Z_P + \mathbf{C}^R_P$$

**Total fuel cost**:
$$\mathbf{C}_F = \mathbf{c}_e + \mathbf{C}_P \cdot \mathbf{F}_P$$

### Unit Costs (Per Unit of Product)

Unit costs represent the cost per unit of exergy:

**Unit exergy cost**:
$$\mathbf{c}^E_P = \mathbf{k}_e \cdot \mathbf{P}$$

where $\mathbf{k}_e = \mathbf{c}_e \circ \mathbf{k}$ is the element-wise product of resource costs and unit consumptions.

**Unit external cost**:
$$\mathbf{c}^Z_P = \mathbf{z}_P \cdot \mathbf{P}$$

where $\mathbf{z}_P$ is the vector of unit external costs (external costs per unit product).

**Unit product cost**:
$$\mathbf{c}_P = \mathbf{c}^E_P + \mathbf{c}^Z_P + \mathbf{c}^R_P$$

**Unit fuel cost**:
$$\mathbf{c}_F = \mathbf{c}_e + \mathbf{c}_P \cdot \mathbf{P}_F$$

## System-Level Metrics

### System Unit Cost

The **system unit cost** represents the overall resource efficiency:

$$k_{\text{system}} = \frac{\sum \text{ResourceExergy}}{\sum \text{FinalDemandExergy}}$$

This metric indicates how much resource exergy is required to satisfy one unit of final demand.

### Recirculation Factor

The **recirculation factor** $r_f$ for each process indicates cost amplification due to circular flows:

$$r_f = \text{diag}(\mathbf{C}_P)^T - \mathbf{1}$$

A high recirculation factor indicates that circular dependencies significantly increase costs.

## Irreversibility Cost Formation

The **irreversibility cost** of process $p$ accounts for the cost of exergy destruction:

$$\text{ICT}_p = (\mathbf{I}_{\text{op}})_p \cdot \mathbf{c}_N$$

where $\mathbf{c}_N$ is the vector of minimum process costs (costs assuming no irreversibilities downstream).

The **total cost including irreversibilities**:

$$\mathbf{c}_{\text{in}} = \mathbf{c}_n + \sum_{p} \text{ICT}_p$$

This decomposition allows identification of which processes contribute most to overall system costs through their irreversibilities.

## Cost Formation Principles

### Direct Cost

Direct cost represents the pure exergy-based valuation:
- Resources valued by their exergy content
- Cost propagates through fuel-product relationships
- Reflects thermodynamic losses and destruction

### Generalized Cost

Generalized cost includes external considerations:
- Capital investment costs
- Operation and maintenance costs
- Environmental impact costs
- Economic externalities

The total generalized cost provides a comprehensive valuation that combines physical (exergy) and economic/environmental factors.

## Theoretical Foundation

The mathematical framework is based on:

1. **Non-negative matrices**: Flow and process matrices have non-negative entries
2. **M-matrix theory**: Ensures operator invertibility under productivity conditions
3. **Spectral radius**: $\rho(\mathbf{K}_P) < 1$ guarantees convergence
4. **Series convergence**: Neumann series $(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{A})^{-1} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \mathbf{A}^k$ for $\rho(\mathbf{A}) < 1$

See [M-Matrix](M-Matrix.md) for detailed mathematical properties.
